How to Get a Job in Cybersecurity

Cyber Career is an online resource guide designed to help students understand the process of entering the cybersecurity field. It provides a structured overview of the key steps required to become job-ready, including earning certifications, gaining internship experience, and learning industry tools. The site organizes these topics into three main sections, each of which contains additional pages that focus on specific resources such as certification providers, job platforms, and commonly used cybersecurity tools.

The content of the site is designed to simplify what can otherwise feel like an overwhelming career path. Instead of presenting too many options, it highlights the most relevant and widely used resources in cybersecurity and explains how each one connects to real-world job requirements. This makes the site especially useful for students who are unsure where to begin or how to prioritize their time when preparing for a career in cybersecurity.

How to use this site effectively

  • Start with the Certifications section if you are new to cybersecurity concepts
  • Move to the Internships section to begin applying for real-world experience
  • Use the Tools section to build hands-on skills that you can show to employers

Certifications → CompTIA

CompTIA

What is CompTIA?

CompTIA (Computing Technology Industry Association) is a certification provider that offers vendor-neutral certifications in information technology and cybersecurity. These certifications — including Security+, Network+, and CySA+ — are designed to teach foundational and intermediate-level skills that can be applied across many different systems and environments. Unlike vendor-specific certifications, CompTIA focuses on general knowledge that is useful regardless of the tools or technologies a company uses.

Popular Certifications

  • Security+ — The most popular entry-level cybersecurity cert globally. Covers threats, attacks, network security, cryptography, and risk management. Required by U.S. DoD contractors under DoD 8570.
  • Network+ — Covers networking fundamentals, infrastructure, and troubleshooting. An excellent prerequisite before Security+, especially if you are new to IT.
  • CySA+ — Focused on threat detection and incident response. Great next step after Security+ for those pursuing SOC or analyst roles.
Security+Network+CySA+PenTest+

Why It's Good for Beginners

CompTIA certifications are designed to be accessible to candidates with limited work experience. The content offered includes structured exam objectives, study materials, and certification tests that cover topics such as networking, threat detection, and security fundamentals. This makes it especially useful for students who are just beginning their cybersecurity journey. Free platforms like Professor Messer offer full exam prep at no cost.

Why Employers Recognize It

Many employers recognize CompTIA certifications, and they are often listed as requirements for entry-level roles, particularly in government and contractor positions. Federal government contractors are frequently required by law to hold Security+ or equivalent under DoD 8570. Private-sector hiring managers treat it as a reliable baseline that filters for serious candidates who have put in the study time.

Tips for Using This Resource

  • Start with Security+ as your first cybersecurity certification
  • Use free resources like videos and practice exams to prepare
  • Treat CompTIA certifications as a foundation before moving to advanced options

Certifications → Offensive Security

Offensive Security

What is Offensive Security?

Offensive Security is a cybersecurity training organization that focuses on hands-on learning and real-world application. Unlike traditional certification providers that rely on multiple-choice exams, Offensive Security requires users to demonstrate their skills in lab environments by completing realistic challenges. Their certifications are designed to test problem-solving ability and technical skill rather than memorization.

Focus on Hands-On Training

The OffSec philosophy is "Try Harder" — a culture of pushing past frustration to develop real problem-solving skills under pressure. Their training platform includes a full course with thousands of practice machines in a dedicated lab network, mirroring what red teamers and penetration testers actually do on the job.

Certifications

  • OSCP (Offensive Security Certified Professional) — The flagship certification. A 24-hour live exam where you must compromise a set of machines and write a professional penetration testing report. Widely considered the gold standard for entry-to-mid pentesting roles.
  • OSEP — Advanced evasion and active directory attack techniques for experienced red teamers.
  • OSED — Exploit development and advanced Windows exploitation techniques.
OSCPOSEPOSED

Why It's Useful for Pentesting / Offensive Roles

The organization is best known for the OSCP certification, which requires users to perform penetration testing tasks in a controlled environment. This makes it highly valuable for students interested in offensive security roles such as ethical hacking or penetration testing. Because of its difficulty and practical focus, it is widely respected by employers in the cybersecurity industry.

Tips for Using This Resource

  • Build a strong foundation in networking and security before starting
  • Practice consistently in labs to develop problem-solving skills
  • Be prepared to spend significant time learning and experimenting

Certifications → SANS Institute

SANS Institute

What is SANS?

The SANS Institute is a cybersecurity training organization that provides advanced education and certifications through its GIAC certification program. It offers a wide range of courses covering topics such as incident response, digital forensics, penetration testing, and cloud security. These programs are taught by experienced professionals and are designed to reflect real-world cybersecurity challenges.

Advanced Training & GIAC Certifications

GIAC (Global Information Assurance Certification) administers exams independently from course attendance, ensuring that certifications reflect genuine mastery. Popular GIAC certifications include:

  • GSEC — GIAC Security Essentials. Broad foundational coverage across security domains.
  • GCIH — Incident Handler certification. Highly valued in SOC and IR roles.
  • GPEN — Penetration Tester. Respected for offensive security roles.
  • GCFE / GCFA — Forensic Examiner certifications for digital forensics tracks.
GSECGCIHGPENGCFA

Why It's Respected in Enterprise & Government

SANS certifications are known for being rigorous and highly respected in both enterprise and government environments. They are often pursued by professionals later in their careers due to their advanced level and cost. However, they provide a strong pathway for specialization and are considered valuable credentials for higher-level cybersecurity roles. Many employers sponsor employees through SANS training because they trust its quality.

Tips for Using This Resource

  • Use SANS as a long-term goal rather than a starting point
  • Look into scholarships or work-study opportunities to reduce cost
  • Choose courses that match your specific career interests and intended specialization

Internships → LinkedIn

LinkedIn

What is LinkedIn?

LinkedIn is a professional networking platform that allows users to connect with others, search for jobs, and build an online presence. It includes features such as job listings, networking tools, and profile-building options that allow users to showcase their experience, certifications, and skills. It is widely used by recruiters and professionals across many industries, making it one of the most important platforms for entering any professional field.

How It Helps Students Find Internships

For cybersecurity students, LinkedIn is especially valuable because it combines job searching with networking opportunities. Users can connect with professionals, follow companies, and discover internship opportunities that may not be listed elsewhere. Job search filters let you target internship roles by discipline, location, company size, and work arrangement — and setting up alerts means you're notified as soon as relevant roles are posted.

Why Networking Matters Here

A large percentage of jobs are filled through referrals before they are publicly posted. LinkedIn is where that process happens. Connecting with cybersecurity professionals, engaging with their content, and reaching out for informational interviews can surface unadvertised opportunities and get your name in front of hiring managers before you ever submit an application.

Tips for Using LinkedIn Effectively

  • Keep your profile complete and up to date — complete profiles rank higher in recruiter searches
  • Connect with professionals in cybersecurity fields using personalized connection requests
  • Add certifications, projects, and lab work to your Experience and Licenses sections
  • Use job alerts to stay informed about new opportunities as soon as they are posted
  • Post about your learning journey to build visibility with recruiters and peers

Internships → USAJOBS

USAJOBS

What is USAJOBS?

USAJOBS is the official job website for the United States federal government and serves as the main platform for finding government employment opportunities. It includes listings for internships, student programs, and full-time positions across a wide range of agencies, including those focused on cybersecurity such as DHS, NSA, CISA, DoD, and the FBI.

How It Helps Students Find Government Internships

This resource is especially useful for students interested in government careers because it provides access to positions that are not typically listed on other job platforms. Programs such as Pathways offer structured opportunities for students to gain experience and transition into full-time roles. The Pathways Internship Program is specifically designed for current students and can lead directly to permanent federal employment after graduation.

Why It's Useful for Federal Cybersecurity Roles

The federal government is the largest employer of cybersecurity professionals in the United States. Federal cybersecurity roles often come with strong benefits, job stability, and the opportunity to work on national-scale security challenges. Many positions also sponsor security clearances — a credential that dramatically expands private-sector opportunities as well.

Tips for Using USAJOBS Effectively

  • Create a complete profile before applying — your resume lives there and feeds into every application
  • Apply early: federal application windows often close quickly and cannot be reopened
  • Read job descriptions carefully to meet all eligibility requirements
  • Use saved searches to track new opportunities as they are posted
  • Search specifically for Pathways Internship Program positions if you are currently enrolled

Tools → Wireshark

Wireshark

What is Wireshark?

Wireshark is a network analysis tool used to capture and examine network traffic. It allows users to view data at the packet level and understand how information flows across a network. This makes it a valuable tool for analyzing communication between systems and identifying potential security issues. It is free, open-source, and used by security professionals worldwide.

How It's Used for Packet Analysis

Wireshark captures raw network packets and decodes them across hundreds of protocols — TCP/IP, DNS, HTTP, TLS, ARP, and more. Analysts use it to reconstruct sessions, identify malicious traffic patterns, detect credential leaks over unencrypted protocols, and analyze malware communication. Its display filter system allows precise focus on specific traffic of interest.

Why It's Important for Networking, SOC, and Forensics

The tool is widely used in cybersecurity roles such as network security, SOC analysis, and digital forensics. It provides insight into how networks operate and helps users detect suspicious or malicious activity. For students, learning Wireshark builds a strong foundation in networking and analysis that translates across multiple career paths.

  • SOC Analysts use Wireshark to investigate alerts and confirm whether activity is benign or malicious.
  • Incident Responders analyze capture files to reconstruct attacker activity.
  • Digital Forensics examiners use captures as evidence in investigations.

Tips for Beginners Using Wireshark

  • Start by analyzing basic network traffic to understand how it works
  • Learn common display filters to make analysis easier and more targeted
  • Practice using sample datasets to build experience before working on live captures
  • Follow TCP streams to reconstruct full conversations between hosts
  • Study how common attack traffic looks: ARP poisoning, port scans, unencrypted credentials

Tools → Splunk

Splunk

What is Splunk?

Splunk is a Security Information and Event Management (SIEM) platform used to collect and analyze log data from different systems. It allows users to search, monitor, and visualize data in order to detect and respond to security threats. It is commonly used in security operations centers to investigate incidents and monitor activity across an organization's infrastructure.

How It's Used in SOC Environments

In a typical SOC, Splunk is the central platform where alerts surface, investigations begin, and threat hunting happens. Security analysts write SPL (Splunk Processing Language) queries to search logs, correlate events across systems, and identify patterns consistent with known attack techniques. Automated alerts fire when anomalies exceed defined thresholds, enabling faster detection and response.

Why It Matters for Log Analysis and Threat Detection

This tool is especially important for students interested in SOC roles because it is widely used in professional environments. Understanding how to use Splunk can make candidates more competitive in the job market and better prepared for real-world cybersecurity tasks. Splunk proficiency is frequently listed as a direct requirement in security operations job postings, making it one of the highest-value tools to learn.

Tips for Beginners Using Splunk

  • Take advantage of free training courses offered by Splunk Education
  • Practice writing queries to search and analyze data — start with index, stats, and table commands
  • Use sample datasets to simulate real-world scenarios before working on live environments
  • Work through the Boss of the SOC (BOTS) datasets for hands-on threat hunting practice
  • Consider the Splunk Core Certified User certification to formalize your knowledge
